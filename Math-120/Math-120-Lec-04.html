When scatterplots make sense (and when they dont)

First, a good example: mpg dataset

mpg %>% head
## # A tibble: 6 x 11
##   manufacturer model displ  year   cyl trans  drv     cty   hwy fl    class
##   <chr>        <chr> <dbl> <int> <int> <chr>  <chr> <int> <int> <chr> <chr>
## 1 audi         a4      1.8  1999     4 auto( f        18    29 p     comp
## 2 audi         a4      1.8  1999     4 manua f        21    29 p     comp
## 3 audi         a4      2    2008     4 manua f        20    31 p     comp
## 4 audi         a4      2    2008     4 auto( f        21    30 p     comp
## 5 audi         a4      2.8  1999     6 auto( f        16    26 p     comp
## 6 audi         a4      2.8  1999     6 manua f        18    26 p     comp
#idea:  compare city and hwy fuel efficiency!

qplot(cty, 
      hwy, 
      data=mpg, 
      geom="point")

This is a clear positive relationship. That makes perfect sense: if a car is efficient in the city, of course its efficient on the hwy.

# try disp and hwy


qplot(displ,
      hwy,
      data=mpg)

This is a negative relationship. Makes sense! Bigger engines are less efficient.

Good Bad Example

# scatterplot of RATE01 and RATE03 (academic ability and compet)

qplot(x=RATE01,
      y=RATE03,
      data = cirpData,
      geom="point")

Woah! What happened? Scatterplots dont make sense for categorical data!! Cant tell how many points!

A possible fix: jitter.

qplot(x=RATE01,
      y=RATE03,
      data = cirpData,
      geom="point")+geom_jitter(width=1/20, alpha = 1/2)
